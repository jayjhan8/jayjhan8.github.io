D-1, Round 3

2018-05-04

These two weeks could not have gone any quicker, but I feel like I haven’t done all that much, not that I am supposed to be doing much anyway. I spent most of my time reading and walking. Rent’s gardening and NBA Playoffs have been fun to watch. But all of the joy is short-lived, and now I must embrace the hospital life again. While I feel healthy once again, I don’t feel as ready as last time. Thinking about that hospital floor makes me nauseous. A friend had asked if this nauseousness is comparable to being seasick or carsick. To anticipate nausea is one thing, but to have it over a period of 10 days is a different beast. I wouldn’t say it is as intense, but it surely lingers for a long time. I can’t wait to be done with this madness.

Tomorrow is the big day. My doctor will go over the scan with me, and he will tell me if I need one or two more cycles. Perhaps I may need surgery or whatever it takes to get this stuff out of me. I’m nervous. So anxious that I have a hard time sleeping. What if this isn’t it? What if there is more? I remain hopeful as I cling onto God’s grace. Do you have a wound so deep that you tried to obliterate from your memory, you wish to be freed from the associated burden? Read this verse with me:

So to keep me from becoming conceited because of the surpassing greatness of the revelations, a thorn was given me in the flesh, a messenger of Satan to harass me, to keep me from becoming conceited.

2 Corinthians 12 2:7

Apostle Paul speaks to his vision for a church, and how thankful he is for a thorn in his body has kept him from his own arrogance. Would you ever be grateful to have a thorn inside your body? I wouldn’t. I’d cry in pain, and drown in misery. So it’s incredibly aspiring to have a witness like Paul who’s focused on one thing and that’s carrying out God’s words. I just finished a book called Between the World and Me by Ta-Nehisi Coates. Incredibly sad and powerful, the letter to his son demonstrates the core essence of being a black person in America. His writing conveys deep love and fear for his son in the world of Dreamers, and his success as a writer isn’t in the writing, but to the struggle, the bedrock of his identity and those of many black people. I don’t mean to reduce one story to another, but Paul and Coates exemplify how each suffering shapes his story. They didn’t stop in the face of hardship, but they pushed forward. Inspired by these stories, I am ready to tackle number 3. Update to follow tomorrow. Pray for me!
