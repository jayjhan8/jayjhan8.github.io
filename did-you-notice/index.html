Did you notice?

2018-04-27

You may notice that the blog looks slightly different, if you haven’t, scroll down. You can now comment on each of my post. I have gotten many requests to get this comment feature implemented, so I hope it works as intended. When I was talking to Sammy earlier, I realized that the audience of my blog has grown pretty quickly. I realize that the point of this blog is for me to tell my story to you, but I figured it’d be nice to hear from you and be more engaging than just my words on the screen. So if you’re reading this post, make sure to drop a comment below. And if you’re up for it, include a song of your all time favorite. I’m just curious to know what jam you would groove to on a Saturday morning.

Back to the blog. First, I apologize for the hiatus. I wasn’t feeling like writing a whole lot this past week. The post-chemo feels weren’t that much more intense, but it’s definitely more persistent. I still feel a tiny bit of nausea, and I think it’s related to how chemo affects your entire GI tract. My esophagus feels a bit tight, and my tastebuds have slightly changed. But I’m still eating really well. Thanks to my mom, I get showered with nutrient-filled, scrumptious Korean food. I also crave to be outside as much as I can. Although I don’t have all that much energy to run, I take alot of walks outside. I actually haven’t explored my neighborhood as much until recently. And everything in my neighborhood is a treat to an eye – the horticulture, modern and old homes, and the parks. I didn’t know there were such nice parks in the area! And since the first day of chemo, I’ve seen the colors change from gray to green, and that’s always exciting to see.

Couple days ago, I found out that I no longer have to shave. Perks of chemo, they say. I had a bit of hair left on my head, so my dad had gotten the razor and went to polish my head. Now, I’m a true baldie. The whole bald thing is interesting, because it’s the only external quality that marks my cancer. I certainly am not used to waking up to a shiny ball of nothingness, but I’m sure no one embraces it at first. It’s a new label, a new identity – I’m sure no one wants to be associated to cancer, but I will always be a cancer patient from now on, in remission or not. I imagine it’s something I’ll look back and tell myself what a crazy experience. It sort of sucks now and I will probably remember this for a long time, but when I look back at this moment years later, I will be able to say that I defeated cancer. And c’mon, that’s pretty badass.
