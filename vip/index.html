VIP

2018-03-24

Tomorrow is the day! I will get admitted around 11AM and I imagine the nurses will work their magic in hooking me up to all the drugs that I need ;). The names of these drugs form my chemotherapy regimen called VIP. I knew I was special, just thought I’d never hear it from a doctor. Nurses have informed me of my potential side effects and what to expect for my treatment. You know, hair loss, low blood cell count, and whatever. Simply put, I’ll be receiving the drugs periodically over the next 5 days while I’m in the hospital – I will be on my merry way to beating cancer.

These past few days, I’ve been reaching out to close friends for prayer and encouragement. I don’t think I’ve ever asked so many people for prayers so openly, but it’s one way I truly feel connected to all the people that surround and encourage me. And right now, I feel more loved and supported than ever before. Going back to the whole VIP shebang, I have come to accept that this is just one of many challenges I’ll face, and eventually overcome. At first, I was frightened by the words cancer and chemotherapy. It almost felt like a bad dream, as if I was one of the few chosen ones for the Hunger Games. But it ironically turns out that I’ve already witnessed so many blessings ever since the diagnosis. I got to reconnect with family and old friends, and felt God stirring in me and those around me. After all, this is a cancer that is curable! Many people don’t get to hear that. Whether it’s 50% or 90%, I’ve got a shot at this whole thing! I don’t know about how you feel, but I really do feel blessed. Even the name of my treatment says so. I felt especially encouraged after one phone call with a stranger whom I now consider a friend. He had undergone chemotherapy and I was only calling to hear his side of the story, but this man had shared with me so much wisdom and positivity like I’ve never felt before. This was just over the phone, too! The most notable part about our conversation was his analogy on chemo to projects. Some projects present a harms way and others don’t, but we all have some projects to complete. So this chemotherapy is just another project. A project that may be difficult, a project that may invoke more projects. Regardless, it’s a project that I will complete until I have no more projects. And with that, I’m going to celebrate a start of a new project tomorrow. One that will be extremely rewarding. Good night!
