Week 1, Day 1 - 3

2018-03-27

Day 1

I got admitted around 11AM, but the actual chemo didn’t start until 7PM. I drew more blood, added a PICC line (a glorified IV), and got a MRI done on my brain just to make sure there is no cancer there. Today is my first day after the 6 full hours of chemo that started around 7PM yesterday and ended around 2AM the following day. I couldn’t really do much because I was so anxious in anticipating what the chemo will do to my body on the first day. Fortunately, I’m not feeling any different than how I did yesterday. Maybe a bit of ligthheadedness, but none really. It’s incredibly hard to explain the sensation of my body undergoing chemo. I think the best way I can describe it is that my mind is really confused. I’m given steroids and anti-nausea meds for a good 30-45m before I get the chemo drugs. Then once these chemo drugs kick in, I slowly get tired as if my body and the meds are constantly fighting each other. It’s just an uneasy feeling. But boy, am I glad that I still don’t have nausea. Did I mention that the hospital I’m staying at has a full on meal service? I had a Pacific Northwest Salmon last night, and I enjoyed myself a lovely linguine with heirloom tomatoes with some focaccia bread spread with basil, pesto, and sun-dried tomatoes for lunch. Mhhm.

Day 2, 3

I felt noticably tired. My appetite is waning and nausea is creeping on me. Day 2 is when I realized I truly have a long battle ahead. But hey, what’s worth a fight if it’s not going to challenge me? Bring it CANCER. In the shower, I thought back to the first half marathon I ran when I was back in school. I was super amped up for it because unlike other races, I got to cross the border to Canada from Detroit and back. Training for it was difficult, but it was nothing like the actual race. I remember having to use the restroom halfway into the race, and my legs were numb probably about 7 miles in. But I didn’t stop running, because I was determined to finish the race without walking. I may have slowed down, but I finished the race with all that I had with the roaring crowd cheering at the finish line. After the shower, I was determined to finish this battle, too. Vomiting or headaches or whatever, I’m going to finish strong.

And chemo isn’t really all that bad if you have amazing people stopping by and cheering you on. I got a handwritten letter from my church and I have been reading that almost everyday. I feel so blessed to have such an army of intercessors pouring out for me. The universal response to my diagnosis from my friends and family is “I’ll be praying for you.” Believers or not, prayer is what everyone resorts to. It’s something that unites all of us in the battle and it’s where I draw my strength and courage from. So keep them coming! We’ll tackle this together :). Onto Day 4!!
